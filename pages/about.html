Yellowstone National Park

This week's park is Yellowstone National Park.

Below is a brief description of the history of Yellowstone as a park, followed by its geologic history, a description of the features of the park, then a description of the wildlife, and finally a story recounting a personal experience in the park. Political history of the park

For at least 10,000 years prior to the first exploration of Yellowstone National Park by people of European origin, it was inhabited by Native Americans. The first explorers of European ancestry known to enter the region arrived in the early 19th century as part of the Lewis and Clark expedition. Over the following 65 years the United States government claimed political control over most of the land in what is now the continiental US, often using violent means to expel Native Americans from the places they had lived. Yellowstone was established as a national park in 1872. Native Americans were expelled from Yellowstone for the most part after it became a national park.

The oldest known evidence of indiginous culture in the Yellowstone has been dated to approximately 11,000 years ago. In the early 1800s, when members of the Lewis and Clark expedition first explored the region that is now Yellowstone National Park, they encountered members of three different tribes; the Nez Perce, Crow and Shoshone. For a time Native Americans and whites coexisted and often traded with each other. As more white explorers settled on native lands violent clashes became more frequent. It was during the 1870s and 1880s that Native Americans were systematically forced to leave Yellowstone by the US Military.

Theodore Roosevelt is known as the father of the National Park Service because he advocated for, and established, many new national parks. However, Yellowstone came into existence many years before Roosevelt was president. It was President Ulysses Grant who signed the law creating Yellowstone National Park as the very first national park in the US. Initially Yellowstone was managed by the US Army. As more national parks were added, especially during Teddy Roosevelt's years in office (1901-1908), the system became unwieldy.

However, it wasn't until 1916 that Woodrow Wilson signed legislation establishing the National Park Service (NPS), creating one system to oversee all of the national parks. Some other famous parks in the system include, Yosemite (in Calfornia), The Grand Canyon (located in Arizona), Bryce Canyon (in Utah), and the closest national park to Yellowstone, The Grand Teton National Park (located less than 100 miles to the south in Wyoming). Yellowstone is primarily in Wyoming, but also extends into Idaho and Montana. In modern times Yellowstone is one of the most visited national parks, with more than 4.2 million visitors in 2016.

Geologic history of the park

Although there are many spectacular characteristics that draw people to the park, Yellowstone is known especially for its hydrothermal features. These include steam vents, fumaroles, hot springs (some of which flow as creeks), colorful hot pools, and of course geysers. The plethora of hydrothermal features is due to the fact that the tectonic plate on which Yellowstone resides is riding over an area where the earth's crust is thin, which in turn forms a geologic hot spot.

The crust is so thin in the area of Yellowstone due to a series of 3 giant volcanic explosions which led to a giant caldera, resulting in the thin crust and other physical features of the park. Each of these eruptions were 1000 times more powerful than the 1980 eruption of Mt. St. Helens. The first of these eruptions was approximately 2.1 million years ago, the second was about 1.3 million years ago, and the most recent eruption was around 640,000 years ago. In many parts of Yellowstone, a large plume of molten magma resides close to the earth's surface. This plume of hot magma in turn superheats ground water which then either seeps to the surface, forming hotsprings, fumaroles, and the many colorful hot pools, or when pressure builds up, the super heated water leads to geyser eruptions intermittently, which occur when the pressure reaches the eruption threshhold.

Geologic features of the park

Three mountain ranges run through the park, with the highest point, atop Eagle Peak, being 11,358 feet. But most of the park consists of the broad plateaus of the caldera formed by the massive volcanic explosion approximately 640,000 years ago. That caldera leads to a very thin crust in the Yellowstone region which is why there are so many geothermal features in the park. Subsequent ice ages led to deep glacier carved valleys and canyons, such as the Grand Canyon of Yellowstone, which in turn led to an even greater thinning of the crust.

Perhaps the best known feature of Yellowstone (or any other national park) is Old Faithful Geyser. Yellowstone actually has more than 300 geysers, which is more than half of the geysers known to exist in the entire world. Steamboat Geyser, the world's largest active geyser, is in Yellowstone. It erupts irregularly, every few months. What makes Old Faithful so famous is that it erupts quite regularly, at an interval of about every 90 minutes.

As noted above, the fact that Yellowstone lies over a geologic hotspot allows for the collection of the superheated water that is constantly seeping from underground to the surface. This in turn is responsible for the hydrothermal features such as the geysers and hot springs, and also over time has led to the beautiful travertine terraces seen in the northwest end of the park.

Wildlife in the park

Yellowstone has more diversity in terms of mammalian wildlife than any other national park in the continental United States, with over 60 different species. There are also many different species of birds, a large variety of fish, as well as a few species of amphibians and reptiles to further add to the diversity of fauna in Yellowstone.

Large mammals often seen in the park include elk, black bears, foxes, and coyotes. Smaller populations of grizzly bears, bighorn sheep, mountain goats and pronghorns (a type of antelope) are also seen in Yellowstone. There are many smaller mammals including hares, river otters, beavers, badgers, martens, and weasels. Of note, while it is important to understand that wild animals can be dangerous, it is fairly uncommon to have injuries or deaths due to human-wildlife interactions. Bears are probably the most feared animal in the park, and yet only 8 people are known to have been killed by bears within the borders of Yellowstone, between 1872 and 2015.

Two large mammals of note are bison and gray wolves. Bison, also known as buffalo, nearly went extinct as a species at the beginning of the 20th century. After agressive protection of these animals, they are now thriving in many parts of North America and there are several thousand bison in Yellowstone National Park alone. Gray wolves had a similar comeback story. They had been virtually eliminated from living in the wild in the entire United States, but were successfully reintroduced in Yellowstone in 1995 and now live throughout the park.

There are over 300 species of birds that spend all or part of the year in Yellowstone. The most common bird species are in the songbird and woodpecker families. Other full-time residents include jays, ravens and Canada geese. The three species of raptors in the park, bald eagles, osprey and peregrine falcons, were all at one time endangered, and all have made great revoveries due to conservation efforts.

Other wildlife in the park include many fish such as cutthroat trout, amphibians such as tiger salamanders, and reptiles such as painted turtles and prarie rattlesnakes. Botany enthusiasts can enjoy more than 1300 different species of flowering plants in the park as well as the large number of trees which are dominated by lodgepole pines and to a lesser extent by other conifer species.

Favorite experiences in the park

In the spring of 1989 I bought a used car in New Hampshire and drove across the country to California. My most anticipated stop of that trip was Yellowstone National Park, a place I wanted to visit primarily to see the geyser called Old Faithful. When I arrived the beauty and majesty of the park surpassed all expectations.

One particularly interesting aspect of my visit was witnessing the destruction that had been visited on Yellowstone by the great fire of 1988. That fire had raged for several months, less than a year before my arrival. Approximately 790,000 acres burned, which is approximately 40% of the park. The vast destruction was impressive but there was also amazing beauty, as colorful wildflowers were blossoming throughout the park, providing a beautiful contrast to the charred remains of tree stumps and shrubs.

Of course 60% of the park was not affected by the fire and there was much unspoiled beauty to be found, for example in the northwest are the travertine terraces discussed above. One especially fun experience for me was to sit in a natural hot tub formed where a natural hot springs, called Hot Water Creek, joined a glacier fed river. Just at the spot of the merger I was able to bathe in water that was very warm, but not too hot.
